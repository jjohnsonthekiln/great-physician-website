Part One of this series established the extraordinary dignity of the human person as the image-bearer of God — crowned with glory, endowed with original righteousness, and oriented toward God as his proper end. But dignity alone does not explain the human condition as it actually presents itself. The person who comes seeking help is not simply a glorious image-bearer in need of encouragement. He is a broken, confused, guilty, and often deeply self-deceived creature who has sinned against God, against others, and against himself.

The doctrine of the image tells us what man was made to be. The doctrine of the fall tells us what happened to it. And it is impossible to care well for a human soul without an honest reckoning with what sin has done to the person who bears it.

A Historical Event with Cosmic Consequences

The Reformed tradition has always insisted that Genesis 3 records a real event in the history of a real man and a real woman — an act of genuine rebellion that had consequences not merely for Adam and Eve but for the entire human race. Calvin is emphatic on this throughout Institutes Book II: the fall is not an illustration of a universal human tendency toward self-assertion; it is the originating act from which that tendency flows.

This historical insistence is not obscurantism; it is the theological precondition for the intelligibility of the gospel. Paul's argument in Romans 5 depends on it entirely: "Just as sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men because all sinned." The symmetry Paul draws between Adam and Christ — the one whose disobedience brought condemnation, the one whose obedience brings justification — requires that both figures be understood as historical heads of the human race whose acts have real consequences for all who are united to them.

The Nature of the First Sin

What was the nature of Adam's sin? The Reformed tradition, following Augustine, has consistently identified its deepest root as pride — but a pride that takes a specific form: the refusal to receive the self as a gift from God, the desire to be autonomous, self-defining, self-sufficient.

Calvin's analysis is precise. The immediate occasion was the serpent's lie — the suggestion that God's prohibition was not an expression of love but a stratagem to deprive humanity of what rightly belonged to it. The first sin was therefore not merely an act of disobedience but an act of unbelief: a refusal to trust God's word, a preferring of the creature's own judgment over the Creator's revealed will. Thomas Watson identifies three dimensions of this sin: ingratitude (committed in the midst of unparalleled blessing), pride (the aspiration to be like God), and unbelief (crediting the serpent's word over God's).

The immediate aftermath of Genesis 3 is a portrait of what sin does to the person who commits it — and any counselor will recognize it. Shame appears first. Then fear: Adam hides from the God whose presence had been his greatest delight, because that presence has now become a source of terror. Then accusation and self-justification: Adam blames Eve; Eve blames the serpent. The pattern of deflection and denial that characterizes human responses to conviction begins at the very moment of the first sin.

Original Sin: Guilt and Pollution

The Reformed tradition teaches that the consequences of Adam's sin did not remain with Adam alone. By virtue of the solidarity of the human race in its covenant head, his sin is imputed to all his posterity — and with it, the corruption of nature sin produced in him is transmitted to every person born by natural generation. This doctrine of original sin has two distinct components.

Guilt: Every human being is born bearing the legal guilt of Adam's transgression. Paul argues in Romans 5 that by the one man's disobedience the many were constituted sinners — not merely affected or influenced, but guilty before God on account of the representative act of the federal head of the race.

Pollution: Every human being is also born with a corrupted nature — what the Westminster Standards call the corruption of the whole nature, the thoroughgoing disordering of every capacity that once bore the image of God in original righteousness. Watson notes pastorally that this inherited pollution is not a tendency toward sin but a positive inclination toward it — not a neutral starting point but a nature inclined toward evil as naturally as water runs downhill. This is why the person who has never understood original sin is often baffled by the intensity of their own sinful desires: why does this feel so strong? Why do I want what I know will destroy me?

Total Depravity: The Whole Person Affected

The Reformed doctrine of total depravity does not mean that every person is as sinful as he could possibly be — or that no genuine goodness remains in fallen humanity. It means that no dimension of the person has escaped sin's corrupting influence. The fall was not a partial event that damaged some faculties while leaving others intact. It reached the whole person.

The mind has been darkened. Paul writes in Romans 1:21 that fallen humanity has become futile in their thinking and darkened in their hearts. In 1 Corinthians 2:14, the natural person cannot accept the things of the Spirit of God because they are spiritually discerned. The noetic effects of sin mean the fallen person cannot reliably evaluate his own spiritual condition or perceive the things of God without the illumining work of the Holy Spirit. Calvin is careful here: the mind retains remarkable capacities for understanding the things of this world — arts, sciences, philosophy, civil life. These are gifts of common grace. But in the things of God, the mind is radically disabled: it needs not more information but illumination.

The will has been enslaved. The fallen will is not neutral between good and evil. It is in bondage to sin — not in the sense that the person is compelled against his inclinations, but that his inclinations themselves are disordered. John 8:34: "everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin." The will chooses freely — but freely according to its nature, and its nature is now corrupted.

The affections have been disordered. The loves of the fallen person are inverted: the things that should be most loved — God, neighbor, truth, righteousness — are feared or despised, while the things that should be properly subordinated — self, comfort, pleasure, reputation — are elevated to the place of supreme devotion. Romans 1:25: they "worshiped and served the creature rather than the Creator."

Why Self-Knowledge Is Unreliable

One of the most practically important implications of total depravity for biblical counseling is the unreliability of fallen self-knowledge. The person who comes seeking help has a version of his own story — a narrative about who he is, why he does what he does, what others have done to him, and what he needs. This narrative is not worthless; it is the place the counselor must begin. But it is not reliable.

"The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?" (Jeremiah 17:9)

Calvin observes that it is precisely the persons most deeply entangled in sin who are often most confident of their own righteousness. The Pharisee's prayer in Luke 18 is not a portrait of spectacular stupidity — it is a portrait of fallen self-knowledge at full development, a person who has curated his self-image so carefully that he genuinely cannot see his own sin. Bavinck connects this to the epistemological crisis of fallen humanity more broadly: the person who does not know God cannot know himself, because the self is only legible in relation to the Creator whose image it bears. Self-knowledge and the knowledge of God are inseparable — the fall damages both simultaneously.

This means that biblical counseling cannot simply accept the counselee's own account of his inner life as accurate. The Word of God — which alone penetrates to the division of soul and spirit (Hebrews 4:12) — is the only instrument capable of cutting through the layers of self-deception that sin has built.

The Deceitfulness of Sin

John Owen, in Of Indwelling Sin, provides the most penetrating Puritan analysis of sin's capacity to hide itself. He identifies several specific mechanisms: sin inflames the imagination before the judgment can evaluate clearly; it minimizes the seriousness of the proposed transgression; it presents delay as a reasonable alternative to repentance; and it exploits the person's genuine spiritual concerns to perpetuate its hold. The counselee has typically not arrived at a state of clear-eyed honesty about his own condition — he has a set of explanations and framings that protect him from the full weight of what his actions have meant. The work of biblical counsel is to bring the light of God's Word to bear with patience, precision, and compassion, trusting the Spirit to do what only he can do.

The Fracture of Relationships

Sin is not only an interior catastrophe — it is a relational one. The image of God is relational all the way down, and sin disrupts every relationship it touches. The primary fracture is with God: Romans 5:10 speaks of the unregenerate as enemies of God, positively aligned against him. The secondary fracture is with the neighbor: the person who does not properly love God cannot properly love his neighbor, because the neighbor is only fully lovable when seen as a fellow image-bearer of the God who commands love.

Genesis 3 enacts this relational fracture with tragic economy. Before the fall, Adam and Eve are together in mutual openness and trust. After the fall, they are clothed, afraid, and accusing one another. The first recorded crime after the fall is fratricide. The pattern from garden to city is one of progressive relational disintegration — the fracture that sin introduced into the relationship with God reproduces itself at every level of human community.

What Remains: Common Grace and the Lingering Image

Total depravity does not mean that nothing good remains in fallen humanity. Calvin's treatment of common grace in Institutes II.2–3 is generous and careful: the intellectual, artistic, and civic achievements of fallen humanity are genuine goods, gifts of the Spirit that should be received with gratitude. Watson calls what remains of the image the ruins of a palace — not the palace itself, but enough of the structure to indicate what the building was meant to be.

For soul care, this means the counselee is not a moral zero. He has a conscience, however suppressed. He has a sense of the moral law, however distorted. He has relational instincts, however corrupted. Biblical counseling works with what remains — not as though it were sufficient, but as the ground on which the Word of God can land.

Why the Diagnosis Must Be Deep Enough

The doctrine of the fall has direct implications for anyone engaged in soul care. The problem is theological before it is psychological — whatever presenting concern a person brings, its deepest root is in the disruption of the relationship with God that sin introduced. Genuine change requires more than information, because the spiritually dead person cannot receive the things of God on his own. The counselor is not the agent of genuine change — the Holy Spirit is. And the gospel must be the destination of every session that reaches the true depth of a person's condition, because what sin has produced cannot be addressed by human wisdom, therapeutic technique, or moral exhortation alone.

Empathy and truth must be joined. Richard Sibbes writes of Christ's tenderness toward the bruised reed — and the bruised reed is not a person who has never sinned, but one who has sinned deeply and is broken under the weight of it. The counselor who has truly internalized the doctrine of total depravity is not hard and unmoved; he is compassionate, understanding that the person before him is in the grip of something far stronger than his own will. And the gentleness of Christ is proportional to the depth of the condition he came to heal.

The Wound and the Physician

"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God." (Romans 3:23)

The magnitude of the fall is measured by the magnitude of what was lost. Part One showed us a creature made in the image of God, crowned with glory, equipped with original righteousness, oriented toward his Creator and his neighbor. Part Two has shown us what happened to that creature — the image defaced, the righteousness lost, the mind darkened, the will enslaved, the affections disordered, the relationships shattered.

This is not a small problem. It is not a problem that education, therapy, or self-improvement can solve. It requires an intervention from outside — from beyond the fallen order, from the One who made the creature and who alone has the power and the love to restore what sin has destroyed.

Part Three — Renewed After the Image — will take up that restoration: what redemption and sanctification accomplish in the person, and what the final renewal of the image in Christ looks like. The wound at the root is real and deep. But the Physician who comes to heal it is greater than the wound.
