Before any question about what ails the human soul — before any diagnosis of sin, any prescription of grace, any act of biblical counsel — there is a prior question that shapes everything else: What is a human being?

It is not an abstract question. It is the most practical question in the world. How you answer it determines what you think the problem is, what you think the solution is, and what kind of help a suffering person actually needs. If the human being is essentially a biological organism shaped by evolutionary pressure, the appropriate response to suffering is adjustment. If the human being is a social construct whose identity is self-determined, the appropriate response is affirmation. But if the human being is a soul made in the image of God, fallen through sin, and redeemable through Christ — the appropriate response is something altogether different: truth, repentance, grace, and the restoration of a broken relationship with the living God.

This three-part series develops that account. Part One concerns the image of God: what it is, what it means, and why it matters for anyone who wants to care well for a human soul.

The Declaration

The biblical account of human origins begins not with a process but with a declaration. Before God forms man, he deliberates — within himself, in the eternal counsel of the Trinity:

"Let us make man in our image, after our likeness." (Genesis 1:26)

This pre-creative deliberation marks humanity as categorically different from everything that preceded it. The other creatures are called into being by command. Man is first considered, then formed — and formed according to a pattern: the divine image. Three things are immediately clear. The image is given, not achieved — it is the Creator's gift, not the creature's accomplishment. It is shared — "male and female he created them" — belonging equally to man and woman. And it comes with a vocation attached: "let them have dominion." To bear the image of God is to be called to represent God's reign in the world he made.

What the Image Actually Is

The Reformed tradition has developed a careful account of what image-bearing actually involves, drawing especially on two New Testament texts. In Colossians 3:10, the renewed self is described as being "renewed in knowledge after the image of its creator." In Ephesians 4:24, it is "created after the likeness of God in true righteousness and holiness." From these, the tradition identifies the original content of the image: true knowledge (the mind rightly oriented toward God), righteousness (the will rightly ordered in conformity with God's law), and holiness (the affections rightly devoted to God as the supreme good).

Calvin, commenting on Genesis 1:26, writes that the image consisted originally in the integrity and uprightness of the whole person — reason illumined by the knowledge of God, the heart set in conformity to his will. Thomas Watson, in his Body of Divinity, summarizes it as wisdom, holiness, and righteousness — three capacities given in full measure to the first human beings, constituting what the tradition calls original righteousness: the proper condition of a creature made for fellowship with a holy God.

But the image also has a broader dimension — the structural features of human personhood that persist even after the fall: rationality, moral agency, language, self-consciousness, and the capacity for genuine relationship with God and others. These are why James can still warn against cursing men "made in the likeness of God" (James 3:9) even in their fallen state. The image is defaced by sin, but it is not destroyed — and this is the ground of the continuing dignity and accountability of every human being.

The Inseparability of Self-Knowledge and the Knowledge of God

Calvin opens the Institutes with one of the most celebrated sentences in Christian theology: "Nearly all the wisdom we possess consists of two parts: the knowledge of God and of ourselves." And he immediately adds that these cannot be separated — each leads to the other, and neither is complete without it.

This is not incidental to the doctrine of the image. It is its epistemological foundation. To know what a human being is, one must know who God is — because the human being is defined by relationship to God. The image is not an autonomous possession that can be understood in isolation. It is a relational reality that only becomes legible when the person recognizes both the source of his dignity (the Creator whose image he bears) and the orientation of his being (toward God as his proper end).

Bavinck develops this from another angle: at the heart of human self-consciousness, he argues, is an awareness of absolute dependence — a recognition, prior to all explicit reflection, that the self does not originate itself. This is the creaturely echo of the Creator's address, the soul's awareness that it exists in relation to One who made it and holds it in being. It is also the reason that no human being is ever truly ignorant of God. The person who comes seeking help may be confused, deceived, or suppressing what he knows — but he is not blank. Every human being carries within himself the marks of image-bearing: conscience, the sense of divinity, the longing for transcendence.

Body and Soul — A Unity, Not a Prison

The image of God is not borne by the soul alone. Genesis 2:7 presents man as a body-soul unity — dust animated by the divine breath, producing a single living person. The Reformed tradition has consistently resisted both extremes in the historical debate: against materialism, insisting that the person is not reducible to his physical components; and against Platonic dualism, insisting that the body is not a prison but a constituent part of the person — made by God, good in itself, and destined for resurrection.

Bavinck puts it precisely: the human being is not a soul that has a body but a body-soul unity — a single person who exists in and through both dimensions at once. Death is consistently presented in Scripture as an unnatural intrusion, a violence done to the created unity of the person. That is why the resurrection of the body is not an appendix to redemption but its culmination.

For anyone engaged in soul care, this matters immediately. The problems a person brings are never merely spiritual in a disembodied sense, nor merely physical in a reductionist sense. They are problems of a person — a body-soul unity whose thoughts, emotions, habits, and physical condition are all intertwined.

The Vocation: Made for Dominion

The image of God is not only a status but a calling. God's first address to the human pair is not a metaphysical description but a commission: "Be fruitful and multiply and fill the earth and subdue it, and have dominion" (Genesis 1:28). The human being is placed as God's vicegerent — the one through whom God's sovereign care for the creation is to be administered and expressed.

This means that work, creativity, intellectual inquiry, artistic production, and the development of civilization are not secular interruptions to the spiritual life but expressions of it — genuine exercises of the image of God in the world. Calvin observes that wherever we find the arts and sciences flourishing, even among unbelievers, we should recognize the common grace of God preserving the gifts of the image in its fallen condition. The image, even after the fall, still reaches toward truth, beauty, and order — because these are the marks of the God in whose image it was made.

The Dignity That Cannot Be Erased

One of the most practically important implications of the doctrine of the image is that it grounds an inviolable human dignity that no circumstance can strip away. The Puritan writers were particularly sensitive to this in their pastoral application. Thomas Boston begins his account of fallen human nature with the state of innocence precisely because he wants readers to understand what was lost — and therefore what remains to be restored.

The person in the depths of addiction, the person paralyzed by guilt, the person whose relationships have shattered under the weight of habitual sin — each is still an image-bearer. The image is defaced and distorted by the fall, but it is not destroyed. It cannot be destroyed, because it is a gift of the Creator, not an achievement of the creature.

Richard Sibbes, in The Bruised Reed, grounds Christ's tenderness toward struggling people in precisely this dignity: "Christ will not break a bruised reed." This gentleness is not sentimental. It is theological. The bruised reed was made in the image of God, bears the marks of that image even in its brokenness, and is the object of a redemptive love that aims at restoration, not merely preservation.

For the biblical counselor — or anyone seeking to help a struggling soul — this establishes what we might call a dignity baseline. Every person who comes seeking help carries a dignity that demands to be honored regardless of the depth or number of their sins. The counselor who has internalized the doctrine of the image does not look at a counselee and see primarily a problem or a project. He sees — beneath all the damage — a person made in the image of God, loved by God, and capable of being restored to God.

The End of the Story

The doctrine of the image looks not only backward to the original creation but forward to its final fulfillment. Paul reveals that the image has a telos — an end it was always oriented toward — that was not fully realized in Adam's original condition. Those whom God foreknew he predestined "to be conformed to the image of his Son" (Romans 8:29). Jesus Christ — the Last Adam, the true and perfect image of the invisible God (Colossians 1:15) — is what Adam was meant to be and never fully became.

The redemptive project is not a return to Eden but an advance beyond it: a conformation of redeemed humanity to the perfect image of the Son. The image given at creation, damaged at the fall, being restored in redemption, will be perfected at the resurrection — when the children of God will be like him, because they will see him as he is (1 John 3:2).

"What is man that you are mindful of him, and the son of man that you care for him? Yet you have made him a little lower than the heavenly beings and crowned him with glory and honor." (Psalm 8:4–5)

The psalmist's astonishment is not naivety. It is theological precision. The dignity of the human being is real, vast, and permanently grounded in the creative act of God — not in human achievement, social recognition, or personal virtue. It is the permanent signature of the Creator on every human life.

This is where any serious account of the human condition must begin: not with dysfunction but with dignity, not with the fall but with the creation. We cannot understand what was lost without first understanding what was given. And what was given was extraordinary.

Part Two will take up the fall — what sin has done to this image, how thoroughly the damage runs, and why no merely human resource can heal it.
