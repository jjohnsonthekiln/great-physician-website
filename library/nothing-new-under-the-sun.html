This essay offers one, drawn not from the latest headlines or the most recent academic commentary on AI ethics, but from a Dutch Reformed theologian who died in 1921 and who saw the crisis coming more clearly than most of his contemporaries.

Herman Bavinck did not write about artificial intelligence. He wrote about something more fundamental: the question of where human knowledge comes from, what it can reach, and what lies permanently beyond its grasp. His answer provides a framework that locates AI precisely on the map of human knowing â€” neither dismissing its genuine power nor mistaking it for something it can never be.

The anxiety and the ancient answer

The concern that something important is at stake with AI is correct. But the sense that nothing like this has ever happened before â€” that we stand at a uniquely unprecedented threshold, that the rules of human history no longer apply â€” that sense is worth interrogating. And the place to interrogate it is Ecclesiastes.

"What has been is what will be, and what has been done is what will be done, and there is nothing new under the sun." (Ecclesiastes 1:9)

The Preacher is not a nihilist. He is saying something precise: the structure of human experience, the kinds of challenges that arise when creatures of finite wisdom build powerful things in a fallen world, the pattern of hope and disappointment that attends every new technology â€” these do not change with the technology. Every generation has had its version of the thing that seemed to transcend all previous limits.

The printing press produced anxiety about the democratization of dangerous ideas. Newtonian mechanics produced anxiety about a clockwork universe with no room for God. Darwin produced anxiety about human dignity. Freud produced anxiety about the self's opacity to itself. The atom bomb produced anxiety about annihilation. In each case the anxiety was not entirely wrong â€” real things were at stake. And in each case the theological question underneath the technological anxiety was the same question: What is the human being, and what does he need that no tool can give him?

Bavinck was writing at one of those waves â€” the cresting of scientific naturalism, when it seemed to many thoughtful people that science would simply replace everything religion had claimed to offer. His response was not defensive. He did not retreat into a protected sphere of spiritual experience and yield the rest to the scientists. He walked straight into the epistemological argument and showed, from within it, where the argument leads â€” and where it cannot go.

The ascent: from sense perception to first principles

Bavinck's argument in Christian Worldview begins exactly where the skeptic begins: with sense perception and the science it makes possible. He does not start with the Bible and deduce downward. He starts on the ground floor of empirical experience and asks what must be true for knowledge to be possible at all.

You begin with sense data â€” the raw material of experience. Science takes this raw data and organizes it through rigorous method into laws and patterns. This is genuine achievement. But press a level deeper and a problem appears. To organize the data into laws, you must already be using something not itself derived from the data: the laws of thought. Logic. Causality. The confidence that the same causes produce the same effects. The assumption that your reasoning faculty corresponds to what is actually true.

Where do these come from? You cannot derive them from sense data, because you must already be using them to evaluate any sense data. You cannot justify them by argument, because every argument already assumes them. And they work â€” in a way that has no explanation within the purely empirical framework. Why does the structure of the mind correspond to the structure of the world? Why does mathematics, developed in pure abstraction, describe physical reality with uncanny precision?

Follow that thread back to its source and you arrive at what Bavinck calls the first principles â€” and behind them, the divine ideas: the eternal archetypes in the mind of God in whom all things were created, and in whom the structures of both the world and the mind that knows it have their common ground. The world is knowable because it was first thought. The laws of logic are reliable because they reflect the structure of the divine mind. The uniformity of nature is grounded in the faithfulness of the Creator.

His conclusion: the three great departments of human knowledge â€” physics, logic, and ethics â€” are not three separate domains that happen to coexist. They are three expressions of a single divine wisdom. This is not a retreat from reason. It is reason followed honestly all the way to its own ground.

AI is exactly what you would expect

Here is where Bavinck's framework becomes directly illuminating for the present moment.

Artificial intelligence is, in the most precise possible sense, the apex of the bottom of Bavinck's diagram. It is sense perception organized, scaled, and automated to a degree unimaginable even a generation ago. A large language model ingests incomprehensible quantities of human-produced text â€” the distilled record of human observation, reasoning, creativity, and expression â€” and finds patterns within it at a depth and scale no human mind could achieve. It is extraordinarily powerful. Its outputs are, in many domains, genuinely useful and sometimes genuinely impressive.

And it cannot, by its own operations, take a single step up Bavinck's diagram. It cannot ask where the laws of logic come from. It cannot reach the first principles that ground its own processing. It cannot arrive at the divine ideas that make sense perception possible in the first place. It is constitutionally and necessarily a creature of the inductive order â€” astonishingly capable within that order, and entirely unable to transcend it.

This is not a criticism of AI. It is a description. And the description is clarifying, because it tells you exactly what AI can do and exactly where it stops.

The anxiety about AI is, at its root, a theological anxiety â€” the uneasy sense that we have built something of enormous power without first principles adequate to govern it or tell us what it is for. That anxiety will not be resolved by better safety guidelines or more transparent training data, though these may be necessary. It will only be resolved by the move Bavinck makes: following the argument all the way up to the ground on which sense perception stands.

The person who has made that move can look at AI and say: yes, of course. This is what a civilization that has become very good at sense perception produces when it scales that capacity to its limit. It is genuinely powerful. It is genuinely limited. And I know both things, because I know where sense perception stands in relation to the God who grounds it.

What sense perception cannot give you

The person in spiritual distress does not primarily need more information. He does not need his sense data better organized. He does not need a more efficient pattern-matching engine applied to his situation. He needs what Bavinck identifies as the content of genuine religion: strength, life, a personal power that can pardon sin, receive him into favor, and cause him to triumph joyfully over a world of sin and death.

Sense perception cannot supply any of this. Science cannot supply it. AI cannot supply it â€” not because it is too weak, but because these things do not live at the level of the inductive order at all. They live at the level Bavinck calls revelation: the self-disclosure of the God who grounds all knowing, addressed to the creature who needs not better data but a restored relationship with the One who made him.

The Word of God is not one data point among many. It is the self-attestation of the One in whom all data has its coherence. When it is spoken to a person in distress, it lands not as an intrusion from outside the system but as the most fundamental statement of how reality actually is â€” because the God who spoke it is the same God whose thought underlies the logic, the physics, and the moral order of the world that person inhabits. The Word lands with power because reality agrees with it. The two were made by the same mind.

Gratitude, proportion, and the gospel

What is the right posture toward AI for the Christian?

Not fear â€” because the God who grounds all sense perception is not threatened by the apex of its development. He was not surprised by the printing press, the microscope, the atom, or the algorithm. There is nothing new under the sun â€” not as a counsel of despair but as a statement of sovereign calm.

Not uncritical enthusiasm â€” because the person who mistakes the bottom of the diagram for everything has confused the instrument with the ground. The one who thinks AI can give him what only God can give him is in the same position as every generation that has made a similar mistake with a different tool.

The right posture is what Bavinck models throughout his work: clear-eyed gratitude. Receive the genuine achievements of sense perception â€” including AI â€” as gifts of the Creator's common grace, products of the created order's God-given intelligibility. Use them freely, wisely, and with full awareness of what they are.

And know that they point, every one of them, toward something they cannot supply. The laws of logic point toward the divine mind. The uniformity of nature points toward the faithfulness of the Creator. The remarkable fact that the world is knowable at all points toward the Word by whom it was made. Every genuine achievement of human knowing is, whether the knower acknowledges it or not, a testimony to the God in whom all knowing is grounded.

That God has not left us with only a diagram. He has entered the world he made. He has spoken in a way that no amount of inductive power could have predicted or produced. He has addressed the condition that no tool can reach â€” the guilt, the broken relationship, the darkened mind â€” with the one thing that can actually heal it: the gospel of his Son.

"For from him and through him and to him are all things. To him be glory forever." (Romans 11:36)

AI is a powerful instrument. The gospel is the destination. And the Christian who keeps those two things in their proper places will be neither afraid of the one nor confused about where to find the other.
