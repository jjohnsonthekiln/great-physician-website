The Image and the Instrument

What AI Can and Cannot Do in the Care of Souls — A Theological Account

The Kiln Ministries · Drawn from Herman Bavinck, Philosophy of Revelation & Christian Worldview

Artificial intelligence is increasingly present in the places where people bring their deepest pain — apps that offer counseling, chatbots that listen without judgment, tools that surface Scripture and pray alongside users in distress. This is not merely a technological development. It is a theological one. It raises questions about human nature, about the nature of knowledge, about what God does in and through the ministry of his Word — and what no machine can do in his place.

The following is a summary of a longer theological argument drawing on the work of the Dutch Reformed theologian Herman Bavinck, whose 1908–1909 Stone Lectures at Princeton Seminary (The Philosophy of Revelation) and his shorter Christian Worldview provide some of the most precise and pastorally useful tools available for thinking through these questions. Bavinck wrote at a moment when scientific naturalism was at its height and the Christian account of human personhood, knowledge, and redemption was under sustained attack. His responses remain, over a century later, exactly suited to the moment we now occupy.

The Problem Beneath the Problem

Bavinck opens Christian Worldview by diagnosing what he calls the defining malady of the modern age: discord. There is a disharmony between thinking and feeling, between willing and acting, between religion and life. The modern self has rejected the integrating center that Christianity provides — the living God who is the source of all being, all truth, and all genuine goodness — and has not found a replacement.

This is the condition that a person brings to any counseling encounter — digital or human. Beneath the presenting anxiety, the relational conflict, the habitual sin, lies a self at war with itself, with the world, and with God. And the question that matters most is this: can a purely immanent resource — one with no access to transcendence, no participation in the divine life, no grounding in the living Word of God — heal this kind of wound?

Bavinck's answer, worked out across both books, is no. Not because such resources are worthless, but because they are categorically unable to reach the root. This is the foundational theological limitation of AI in soul care: it is an immanent instrument. The source of spiritual healing lies outside it and entirely beyond its reach.

The Machine and the Image-Bearer

One of Bavinck's most important philosophical distinctions is between a machine and an organism. A machine's purpose is always external to it — determined by its maker, never taken up into itself as a living principle. An organism, by contrast, moves toward its God-given end from within itself. Where a machine is directed toward its purpose from outside by its maker, an organism carries its God-given direction as an immanent principle — striving, developing, growing toward the end for which it was made.

The human person is not a machine. Made in the image of God, oriented by nature toward fellowship with God, the human being has a telos — an end that is not imposed from outside but written into the fabric of what he is. He can be broken by sin. He can be restored by grace. He can grow in wisdom, be conformed to the image of Christ, be made genuinely new.

A language model has none of this. Its purpose is entirely external to it. It has no nature that strives from within. It cannot grow in wisdom, be sanctified, or bear the image of Christ — because it does not bear that image and never could. The gap between image-bearer and artifact is not a gap of capability. It is a gap of being.

What Genuine Knowledge Requires

In Philosophy of Revelation, Bavinck argues that genuine human knowledge is only possible because God first knew all things, expressed that knowledge in creation, and gave specific form to it in Scripture. The forms of being, the laws of thought, and the norms of conduct all have their common source in divine wisdom. This is what makes the counselor's words carry weight: when truth is spoken, it corresponds to the structure of reality as God made it, and the Word of God — grounded in the same divine wisdom that underlies creation — does not fall void.

AI generates outputs from patterns in human-produced text. Its outputs are not anchored in the self-attesting Word of God, are not illuminated by the Holy Spirit, and are not subject to the living conscience that orients human knowing to moral reality. It can quote Scripture accurately. It cannot apply the Word with the discernment that flows from genuine fear of the Lord.

Religion Is Necessarily Supernatural

Lecture 6 of Philosophy of Revelation provides a point that is easy to overlook: Bavinck's argument that all genuine religion is irreducibly supernatural. There is no middle ground. "Religion is everywhere a service of God," he writes, "and hence it is either folly or necessarily implies the existence of God." The attempt to ground religion in purely immanent factors — psychological need, evolutionary adaptation, social belonging — reduces it to something that can no longer do what religion exists to do.

What does the person in spiritual distress ultimately need? Bavinck's answer, drawn from his survey of what all genuine religion seeks, is clear:

"Strength, life, a personal power that can pardon sin, receive us into favor, and cause us to triumph joyfully over a world of sin and death."

An AI system cannot offer this. It can describe it. It can point toward it. But it cannot be the channel through which the Spirit of God works pardon and new life in a human soul. That work belongs to the living Word, administered through living persons, in the context of living community.

Conversion and the Limits of Psychology

In his lecture on revelation and religious experience, Bavinck makes a pointed observation about the psychology of religion — the emerging academic discipline of his day that sought to explain conversion, faith, and religious experience in purely naturalistic terms. He affirms that such study has its value. But it can observe the phenomenon of conversion from the outside; it cannot penetrate to its cause.

"What conversion is and ought to be," he writes, "no psychology of religion can teach us; the Scriptures alone can tell us that." And the Scriptures are clear: genuine conversion is "the dying of the old sinful man, and the resurrection of the new, holy man" — a work of God, not a product of information exchange.

This is exactly the epistemological limit of any AI counseling system. It can describe conversion. It cannot mediate it. It can present the conditions under which repentance becomes possible. It cannot be the instrument through which the Holy Spirit brings a person from death to life. The Spirit works through means of his own choosing — Word, prayer, the ministry of his people. AI itself is not among the ordained means through which he has promised to work; the counselee who mistakes a digital tool for a channel of grace has confused the instrument with the source. But God remains sovereign over all things, and no tool is outside his providence.

Technology Is a Legitimate Tool — Within Its Limits

None of this is an argument against the use of AI in ministry contexts. Bavinck's theology of creation includes a robust account of human cultural activity — including the development of tools and technology — as a legitimate and God-glorifying expression of the dominion mandate. Technology can serve genuinely good purposes within its proper limits.

The Great Physician application is built with this principle in mind. Its AI counselors are tools — instruments through which the truths of Scripture and sound biblical wisdom can be made accessible, searchable, and conversationally engaging. They are not pastors. They carry no spiritual authority, offer no sacramental presence, and make no claim to replace the ministry of Word, prayer, and Christian community.

But a tool that claims to be more than a tool does not become a better tool — it becomes a dangerous deception. An AI that presents itself as a spiritual guide, a confessor, or a source of grace has overstepped not only its design but the created order itself. Every feature of the Great Physician application is designed to resist this overreach: to be genuinely useful, genuinely humble, and always pointing beyond itself toward the Physician who actually heals.

A Warning About False Eschatologies

Bavinck's final lecture in Philosophy of Revelation surveys the optimistic movements of his day — evolutionary progress, socialist transformation, educational reform — and names their common structure: they are secular eschatologies. They offer salvation without the cross, healing without grace, a future state of human wholeness achieved by human effort alone.

"The doctrine of evolution," he writes, "affords no rest for the mind and none for the heart, because it takes away from us the Lord of the world."

The same warning applies to AI. There is a growing tendency to treat AI as a kind of therapeutic messiah — always available, infinitely patient, free from the failures and limitations of human helpers. For people who have been wounded by institutions, by pastors, by family, this appeal is understandable and real. But it is precisely the kind of false hope Bavinck identifies: seeking in an immanent instrument what can only be found in the transcendent Lord.

The person in spiritual distress needs righteousness, peace, and joy — not better information retrieval. These are gifts of the Spirit, fruits of union with Christ through faith. No application, however carefully designed, can give them. What it can do — what the Great Physician application tries to do — is clear a path toward the One who can.

The Physician and His Instruments

Bavinck closes Philosophy of Revelation with a vision of Christ as the center and fulfillment of all revelation — the one in whom the Creator and the creature meet without confusion, in whom all the great fractures of human existence are at last healed: the fracture between knowing and being, between identity and change, between conscience and conduct.

It is fitting that this application bears his name. The Great Physician diagnoses the deepest disease — sin and its alienation from God — and provides the only cure. This application does not claim to be him. It claims only to point toward him: to use the legitimate tools of human ingenuity and digital communication to bring people into contact with the Word that heals, the community that sustains, and the Physician who saves.

This is the appropriate posture of all technology in the service of the gospel: humble, transparent, instrumental, and always pointing beyond itself to the One who is not a tool but the Lord.
