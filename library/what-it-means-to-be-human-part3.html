Part One showed us what the human being was made to be: an image-bearer of God, crowned with glory, endowed with original righteousness, and oriented toward God as his proper end. Part Two showed us what happened to that creature in the fall: the image defaced, the righteousness lost, the mind darkened, the will enslaved, the affections disordered.

The wound is real and deep. But it is not the last word.

Part Three takes up what the Reformed tradition calls the ordo salutis — the order of salvation — and asks what God does, in Christ, to address the catastrophe of the fall. Not merely to forgive it, though forgiveness is essential. Not merely to restrain its effects, though God’s common grace does that. But to reverse it: to renew the image, restore the righteousness, heal the relationship, and bring the creature to a glory that exceeds even the original condition in Eden.

A new heart

The redemptive process does not begin with human effort. It begins with a sovereign act of God that addresses the deepest level of the person’s incapacity — the spiritual death identified in Part Two as the fundamental condition of the unregenerate. Before a person can repent, believe, or seek God, God must act.

Ezekiel 36:26–27 states it precisely: “I will give you a new heart, and a new spirit I will put within you. And I will remove the heart of stone from your flesh and give you a heart of flesh. And I will put my Spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes.” Three things are promised: the removal of the old heart, the gift of a new heart, and the indwelling of the Spirit who enables genuine obedience. The initiative is entirely divine. The language is entirely transformative. This is not renovation but re-creation.

Jesus speaks of this as being born from above (John 3:3–8). The birth metaphor is deliberate: birth is something that happens to the person. The infant does not cause its own birth; it is born. So the regenerate person does not cause his own regeneration; he is made alive (Ephesians 2:5) by the sovereign act of the Spirit. Calvin, in Institutes III.3, is emphatic: regeneration is not the improvement of the old self but the death of it — and a genuine new creation in its place.

Justification: the guilt addressed

Part Two established that the fallen person bears two burdens: guilt (the legal liability of sin before a holy God) and pollution (the corruption of nature that sin has produced). Justification addresses the guilt. It is a forensic act — a declaration, not a process — by which God pronounces the sinner righteous on the basis of Christ’s imputed righteousness received through faith.

Calvin defines justification in Institutes III.11 as the acceptance of a person by God, who receives him into his favor and regards him as righteous — not on account of any righteousness of his own but solely on account of the righteousness of Christ reckoned to him by grace through faith. This reckoning is complete, immediate, and permanent. The justified person is not on probation; he stands before God in the full righteousness of the Son of God.

For biblical counseling, justification is the answer to the shame and guilt that so often immobilize the counselee. The person paralyzed by past sin needs not reassurance that his sins were not so serious, nor encouragement to try harder, but the objective reality of justification: that in Christ, his guilt has been addressed completely and finally at the cross. The counselor who grounds hope in the accomplished work of Christ rather than the developing work of the counselee gives a foundation that does not shift with the counselee’s performance.

The Last Adam and the true image

The redemptive project as Paul conceives it is not simply the repair of damaged humanity. It is the completion of what humanity was always meant to become — in Christ, the Last Adam, who is the true and perfect image of the invisible God.

Adam was the federal head of the old humanity, whose disobedience brought condemnation to all who are in him. Christ is the Last Adam — the federal head of the new humanity, whose obedience brings justification and life to all who are in him. But Paul’s argument implies something more than symmetry. The Last Adam does not merely restore what the first Adam lost; he brings humanity to a glory that exceeds the original condition. Adam bore the image of the man of dust; the redeemed will bear the image of the man of heaven (1 Corinthians 15:49).

Christ is “the image of the invisible God” (Colossians 1:15) and “the exact imprint of his nature” (Hebrews 1:3). He is what Adam was created to image and never fully became. The goal of redemption is stated with crystalline clarity in Romans 8:29: those whom God foreknew he predestined “to be conformed to the image of his Son.”

Union with Christ

The benefits of redemption — regeneration, justification, sanctification, glorification — are all grounded in a single reality: union with Christ. Calvin, in Institutes III.1, insists that Christ himself is the ground of all that he gives: as long as Christ remains outside of us, all that he has suffered and done remains useless and of no value for us.

To be in Christ is the fundamental description of the believer’s condition throughout Paul’s letters. It is union with Christ that grounds justification (his righteousness is imputed to those in him), union with Christ that grounds sanctification (his Spirit conforms them to his image), and union with Christ that grounds glorification (those in him will be raised as he was raised). For the counselee who has come to faith, this means he is not approaching God as a probationer or a stranger. He is approaching God in Christ — covered by his righteousness, indwelt by his Spirit, and guaranteed his inheritance.

The renewed image: what redemption repairs

Part One identified the narrower image as consisting in true knowledge, righteousness, and holiness — drawn from Colossians 3:10 and Ephesians 4:24. Part Two showed these were precisely what the fall destroyed. Part Three shows that these are precisely what redemption restores.

Colossians 3:10 — “the new self, which is being renewed in knowledge after the image of its creator” — establishes that the renewal of the image is both a present reality and an ongoing process. The new self has been put on (completed act) and is being renewed (continuing process). Both are simultaneously true: the decisive break has been made, and the progressive renewal continues.

Watson’s definition of sanctification is precise: it is “a gracious work of God’s Spirit, whereby, renewing our natures and writing his law in our hearts, he enables us to die unto sin and live unto righteousness.” Three elements: the Spirit’s work, the renewal of nature (not merely the modification of behavior), and the twin movement of mortification and vivification.

Killing sin: Owen on mortification

Owen’s axiom is worth quoting in full: “Be killing sin or it will be killing you.” Indwelling sin does not remain static in the believer; it is active, seeking occasions, exploiting every weakness. The new birth creates a new principle of life — but the old principle is not simply extinguished. The remainder of this life is, as Paul describes in Galatians 5:17, a conflict between the flesh and the Spirit.

Mortification, for Owen, is not self-laceration or mere willpower. It is the Spirit’s work applied through specific means: genuine self-examination, the application of specific gospel truth to the specific temptation, and Spirit-empowered resistance in the moment. But mortification must always be accompanied by vivification — the active cultivation of the corresponding grace. You do not merely stop being proud; you actively cultivate humility. The negative and positive movements belong together, because the renewed image is not merely the absence of vice but the presence of genuine virtue.

For biblical counseling, this transforms the typical counseling encounter. The question is not merely “what are you doing wrong?” but “what grace are you failing to cultivate?” Sin and grace are always mirror images of each other — and the positive cultivation is just as much the counselor’s work as the negative restraint.

The remaining struggle

One of the most practically important doctrines for biblical counseling is the believer’s remaining corruption. The new birth is real. Justification is complete. And yet, as Paul testifies in Romans 7, the struggle with indwelling sin continues: “For I do not do what I want, but I do the very thing I hate.”

This doctrine matters for counseling in two ways. First, it prevents the counselee from concluding that ongoing struggle is evidence of the absence of grace. The very conflict Paul describes in Romans 7 is itself evidence of spiritual life — a spiritually dead person does not war against sin; he simply lives in it. The counselor who recognizes this can offer genuine hope to the counselee devastated by ongoing struggle.

Second, it prevents false expectations about the pace of sanctification. Progress is real and is to be expected — but it is progressive, not instantaneous. Lifelong patterns of sin are not typically broken overnight. The counselor who communicates both the reality of progress and the realism of struggle gives the counselee the fullest picture of what the Christian life actually looks like.

The community of renewal

Sanctification does not take place in isolation. Ephesians 4:11–16 locates the ordinary work of redemptive renewal within the community of the people of God, gathered around Word and sacrament, governed by elders, knit together in genuine love and mutual accountability. The goal is stated in image-bearing terms: “the measure of the stature of the fullness of Christ” — achieved through the mutual ministry of the whole body.

Calvin’s treatment of the church in Institutes IV.1 is direct: there is no other way to enter into life unless this mother conceive us in her womb, give us birth, nourish us at her breast, and keep us under her care until we become like the angels. The church is not a voluntary association of like-minded believers; it is the God-ordained community within which the Spirit does his ordinary work of maturation.

For biblical counseling, this means the counselee cannot be genuinely served by a process that replaces the church. However faithful the counsel, the counseling relationship cannot do what only the church can do. The counselor who genuinely cares for the counselee’s spiritual formation will always be directing him toward genuine participation in a local body — toward the preaching of the Word, the sacraments, the fellowship of believers, and the accountability of pastoral oversight.

Glorification: the completion of the image

The renewal of the image that begins in regeneration and continues in sanctification will not reach its completion in this life. It reaches its completion in glorification — the final act of redemption in which the redeemed are fully and permanently conformed to the image of the Son.

Paul’s great arc in Romans 8:29–30 ends with glorification in the past tense — so certain is its fulfillment. The content is stated in 1 John 3:2: “We know that when he appears we shall be like him, because we shall see him as he is.” The vision of Christ in his glory is itself the instrument of the final transformation. The image that was distorted by sin, being progressively renewed by the Spirit in this life, will be perfected in the direct vision of the One whose image it was always meant to bear.

The resurrection of the body is the completion of the redemption of the whole person. The body–soul unity that God created at the beginning, which sin fractured at the fall and which death temporarily tears apart, will be reunited in glory — transformed, perfected, and incorruptible. Bavinck, in Christian Worldview, closes his account of the Christian resolution of the great fractures of human existence with this eschatological vision: the discord between thinking and being, being and becoming, becoming and acting will be fully resolved only at the consummation. In Christ, the resolution has begun. In glory, it will be complete.

The story that ends in magnificence

“And we all, with unveiled face, beholding the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image from one degree of glory to another. For this comes from the Lord who is the Spirit.” (2 Corinthians 3:18)

Three installments. One story. A creature made in the image of God — glorious in his original condition, ruined in the fall, renewed in redemption, and destined for a glory that exceeds everything that came before.

Part One showed us the extraordinary dignity of the image-bearer. Part Two showed us the catastrophe of the fall. Part Three has shown us the remedy: not a patch applied to a damaged surface, but a re-creation from within — regeneration giving life to the dead, justification lifting the guilt of the condemned, sanctification progressively renewing the image through the Spirit’s work over a lifetime, and glorification completing the work when the children of God are fully conformed to the image of the Son.

This is what it means to be human — not merely what we are, but what we were made to be, what we have become through the fall, and what we shall be through the grace of the One who entered the ruin himself, bore the full weight of it on the cross, and rose from it with a resurrection that guarantees our own.

The Great Physician did not come to treat a broken machine. He came to restore a ruined image-bearer. And he leaves no case unfinished.

“He who began a good work in you will bring it to completion at the day of Jesus Christ.” (Philippians 1:6)
