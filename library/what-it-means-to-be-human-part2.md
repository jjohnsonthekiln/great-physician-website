## Preface: Why the Fall Matters

Part One of this series established the extraordinary dignity of the human person as the image-bearer of God — crowned with glory, endowed with original righteousness, oriented toward God as his proper end and rest, and commissioned to exercise royal stewardship over the created order. We saw that this dignity cannot be erased even by sin, and that it establishes the irreducible worth of every person who comes to a biblical counselor seeking help.

But dignity alone does not explain the human condition as it actually presents itself. The person who sits across from a counselor is not simply a glorious image-bearer in need of encouragement. He is a broken, confused, guilty, and often deeply self-deceived creature who has sinned against God, against others, and against himself — and who, in many cases, does not fully understand the nature or depth of his own condition. The doctrine of the image tells us what he was made to be. The doctrine of the fall tells us what happened to it.

Part Two takes up that catastrophe. It is impossible to care well for a human soul without an honest reckoning with what sin has done to the person who bears it. The Puritan tradition was particularly clear on this point. Thomas Boston structures his account of *Human Nature in Its Fourfold State* by moving through innocence, corruption, grace, and glory in deliberate sequence — because each state is only intelligible in light of what preceded it. You cannot understand the corruption without the innocence. You cannot understand the grace without the corruption. And you cannot understand what a person needs without understanding, honestly and without sentimentality, the nature and extent of what ails him.

The biblical account of sin is not pessimism about human nature. It is realism about the human condition — a realism grounded in the seriousness of God's holiness, the clarity of God's law, and the inexorable consequences of the creature's rebellion against its Creator. And it is, paradoxically, the necessary foundation of genuine hope: for only a diagnosis that reaches the true disease can point toward the true cure.

## I. The Fall: A Historical Event with Cosmic Consequences

The Reformed tradition has always insisted, against every form of mythological or allegorical interpretation, that Genesis 3 records a real event in the history of a real man and a real woman — an act of genuine rebellion that had consequences not merely for Adam and Eve but for the entire human race and for the created order itself.

Calvin, throughout *Institutes* Book II, is emphatic on this point. The fall is not an illustration of a universal human tendency toward self-assertion; it is the originating act from which that tendency flows. It happened; it was inexcusable; and its consequences were total. Everything that followed — every sin in every human heart across every subsequent generation — traces its origin to this event and to the solidarity of the human race in the one who committed it.

Bavinck develops the same insistence in his *Reformed Dogmatics*. The doctrine of original sin stands or falls with the historicity of the fall. If Adam's sin is merely a symbol of the human condition, then the human condition has no explanation beyond itself — it is simply what we are, with no alternative account of what we were meant to be or what has gone wrong. But if the fall is historical, then the human condition is not the original condition. It is a disruption of the original condition — a catastrophe that invaded a good creation and produced consequences that no merely human effort can reverse.

This historical insistence is not a piece of obscurantist biblicism; it is the theological precondition for the intelligibility of the gospel. Paul's argument in Romans 5:12–21 depends on it entirely: *"Therefore, just as sin came into the world through one man, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men because all sinned."* The symmetry Paul draws between Adam and Christ — the one whose disobedience brought condemnation, the one whose obedience brings justification — requires that both figures be understood as historical heads of the human race whose acts have real consequences for all who are united to them.

## II. The Nature of the First Sin: Pride, Unbelief, and the Rejection of the Creator

What was the nature of Adam's sin? The Reformed tradition, following Augustine, has consistently identified its deepest root as *pride* — but a pride that takes a specific form: the refusal to receive the self as a gift from God, the desire to be autonomous, self-defining, self-sufficient.

Calvin's analysis in *Institutes* II.1 is precise. The immediate occasion of the fall was the serpent's lie — the suggestion that God's prohibition was not an expression of love but a stratagem to deprive humanity of what rightly belonged to it. The first sin was therefore not merely an act of disobedience but an act of *unbelief*: a refusal to trust God's word, a preferring of the creature's own judgment over the Creator's revealed will. Behind the act of eating the forbidden fruit lay a prior act of the will — the choice to treat God as a rival rather than a Father, as a restricting power rather than the source of all good.

Watson, in his *Body of Divinity*, identifies three dimensions of the first sin that together reveal its comprehensive character. It was an act of *ingratitude* — committed in the midst of unparalleled blessings, against a God who had given everything. It was an act of *pride* — the aspiration to be "like God, knowing good and evil" (Genesis 3:5), the creature's assertion of independence from its Creator. And it was an act of *unbelief* — the crediting of the serpent's word over God's word, the inversion of the epistemological order on which all genuine human knowledge depended.

Boston, in *Human Nature in Its Fourfold State*, adds an analysis of the spiritual dynamics at work in the fall that is particularly instructive for soul care. The will, he argues, was not compelled by external force — it chose freely. But it chose under the distorting influence of a desire that had already been corrupted by the serpent's suggestion. This is the pattern that sin follows in every subsequent temptation: the desires are inflamed by a false presentation of the good, the judgment is darkened by the desire's pressure, and the will chooses what the corrupted affections have made to appear desirable. Understanding this sequence is essential for anyone seeking to help a person trapped in patterns of habitual sin.

## III. The Immediate Consequences: Shame, Fear, and the Broken Relationship

The immediate aftermath of the fall in Genesis 3 is a portrait of what sin does to the person who commits it — and it is a portrait that any counselor will recognize.

*Shame* appears first. *"Then the eyes of both were opened, and they knew that they were naked, and they sewed fig leaves together and made themselves loincloths"* (Genesis 3:7). The nakedness they had known before the fall without shame (Genesis 2:25) now becomes intolerable. The vulnerability of the body becomes a symbol of the vulnerability of the soul — exposed, disordered, knowing that something has been violated that cannot be repaired by human means.

*Fear* appears next. When God comes to walk in the garden in the cool of the day, Adam hides. *"I heard the sound of you in the garden, and I was afraid, because I was naked, and I hid myself"* (Genesis 3:10). The relationship with God that had been the ground of all joy, security, and dignity has now become a source of terror. The presence of a holy God, which should have been the creature's greatest delight, is now the thing most to be avoided. This is the inversion that sin produces at the deepest level of the person: what was made to be the soul's supreme good becomes the soul's supreme dread.

*Accusation and self-deception* follow immediately. When confronted by God, Adam blames Eve; Eve blames the serpent. The pattern of deflection, denial, and self-justification that characterizes human responses to conviction begins immediately at the moment of the first sin. Calvin observes that this self-protective response is not merely a secondary result of sin but an expression of the same pride that produced the sin in the first place. The person who will not acknowledge his guilt before God is still insisting, in his own way, on his own self-sufficiency.

For the biblical counselor, these three immediate consequences of the fall are among the most commonly presenting features of the counselee's condition: the shame that cannot be talked away, the fear of genuine encounter with God, and the elaborate self-justifications that protect the person from the honest confrontation with his own guilt that healing requires.

## IV. Original Sin: Guilt and Pollution in All of Adam's Posterity

The Reformed tradition teaches that the consequences of Adam's sin did not remain with Adam alone. By virtue of the solidarity of the human race in its covenant head, Adam's sin is imputed to all his posterity — and with it, the corruption of nature that sin produced in him is transmitted to every human being born of natural generation.

This doctrine — *original sin* — has two distinct components that must be clearly distinguished.

**Guilt** (*reatus*): Every human being is born bearing the legal guilt of Adam's transgression. Paul's argument in Romans 5:12–19 is that *"by the one man's disobedience the many were made sinners"* — not merely affected, not merely influenced, but constituted sinners by imputation. This is the judicial dimension of original sin: every person stands before God not merely as a sinner on account of his own acts but as a guilty party on account of the representative act of the federal head of the race.

**Pollution** (*corruptio*): Every human being is also born with a corrupted nature — what the Westminster Standards call *the corruption of his whole nature* and what Bavinck describes as the thoroughgoing disordering of all the capacities that once bore the image of God in its original righteousness. The mind, will, and affections that were created in true knowledge, righteousness, and holiness are now, by nature, darkened, enslaved, and disordered.

Watson's treatment of original sin is pastorally astute: he notes that this inherited pollution is not merely a tendency toward sin but a positive inclination toward it — not a neutral starting point that might go either direction with equal ease, but a nature that is *inclined* toward evil as naturally as water runs downhill. The person who has never reflected on the doctrine of original sin will often be baffled by the intensity of their own sinful desires — why does this pull feel so strong? Why do I want what I know will destroy me? The doctrine of original pollution explains what otherwise appears inexplicable.

## V. Total Depravity: The Extent of Sin's Reach

The Reformed tradition's doctrine of *total depravity* is among its most misunderstood contributions. It does not mean that every human being is as sinful as he could possibly be, or that there is no genuine goodness anywhere in fallen humanity. What it means is that no dimension of the human person has escaped the corrupting influence of sin — the fall was not a partial event that damaged some faculties while leaving others intact. It reached the whole person: mind, will, affections, conscience, and body.

**The mind** has been darkened. Paul describes the Gentile world in Romans 1:21 as having become *"futile in their thinking"* and *"darkened in their foolish hearts."* In 1 Corinthians 2:14, he writes that *"the natural person does not accept the things of the Spirit of God, for they are folly to him, and he is not able to understand them because they are spiritually discerned."* The noetic effects of sin are not peripheral; they reach the understanding itself, so that the fallen person cannot reliably evaluate his own spiritual condition, rightly interpret his own experience, or perceive the things of God without the illumining work of the Holy Spirit.

Calvin develops the noetic effects of sin with particular care. The mind retains, after the fall, remarkable capacities for understanding the things of this world — the arts, the sciences, philosophy, civil life. These are gifts of common grace that should be acknowledged and honored. But in the things of God — in the knowledge of God's character, of human sinfulness, of the way of salvation — the mind is radically disabled. It does not merely need more information; it needs illumination, a supernatural work of the Spirit that restores the capacity to see what the fall has made invisible.

**The will** has been enslaved. This is the point at which the Reformation's conflict with Erasmus was sharpest, and where Calvin's *Institutes* Book II presses hardest. The fallen will is not neutral between good and evil, free to choose either with equal facility. It is in bondage to sin — not in the sense that the person is compelled against his inclinations, but in the sense that his inclinations themselves are disordered, and he cannot, by his own natural power, incline himself toward God. John 8:34 establishes the principle: *"everyone who practices sin is a slave to sin."* The will chooses freely — but freely according to its nature, and its nature is now corrupted.

Bavinck develops this from the angle of the creature's proper orientation. The human will was made to find its proper freedom in conformity with the divine will — the very freedom that the angels unfallen and the redeemed in glory enjoy. Sin inverted this: what the person now calls freedom is the assertion of the self against God, and what feels like bondage is the very conformity to God's will that is the creature's proper life. The emancipation that the gospel offers is not liberation from God's will but liberation from the corruption that has made his will feel like a burden.

**The affections** have been disordered. The loves of the fallen person are inverted: the things that should be most loved — God, neighbor, truth, righteousness — are feared or despised, while the things that should be properly subordinated — self, comfort, pleasure, reputation — are elevated to the place of supreme affection. Paul's description in Romans 1:25 — *"they exchanged the truth about God for a lie and worshiped and served the creature rather than the Creator"* — identifies this disordering of the affections as the fundamental spiritual pattern of fallen humanity.

John Owen, in *Of Indwelling Sin in Believers*, provides the most penetrating Puritan analysis of this disorder. He identifies sin's law in the members — the persistent pull of the flesh against the spirit even in the regenerate — as an active, intelligent, deceptive principle that works through the affections, inflaming desire, distorting judgment, and exploiting every weakness of the person in its effort to produce sinful action. Owen's treatment is not pessimistic but diagnostic: the person who understands how indwelling sin works is far better equipped to resist it than the person who is bewildered by its power.

## VI. The Noetic Effects of Sin: Why Self-Knowledge Is Unreliable

One of the most practically important implications of the doctrine of total depravity for biblical counseling is the unreliability of fallen self-knowledge. The person who comes seeking help has a version of his own story — a narrative about who he is, why he does what he does, what others have done to him, and what he needs. This narrative is not worthless; it is the place the counselor must begin. But it is not reliable. It has been shaped by the same fallen mind, disordered affections, and self-protective will that produced the behaviors being counseled.

Calvin, commenting on the self-deception of fallen humanity, notes that it is precisely the persons most deeply entangled in sin who are often most confident of their own righteousness. The Pharisee's prayer in Luke 18 — *"God, I thank you that I am not like other men"* — is not a piece of spectacular spiritual stupidity. It is a portrait of fallen self-knowledge at full development: a person who has constructed a self-image so carefully curated to exclude his own sin that he genuinely does not see it.

Bavinck, in *Philosophy of Revelation*, connects this to the broader epistemological crisis of fallen humanity. The person who does not know God — or who suppresses the knowledge of God that he has (Romans 1:18) — cannot know himself reliably, because the self is only legible in relation to the Creator whose image it bears. Self-knowledge and the knowledge of God are inseparable, as Calvin observed. The fall does not merely damage one of these; it damages both simultaneously.

Jeremiah 17:9 states it with stark economy: *"The heart is deceitful above all things, and desperately sick; who can understand it?"* The rhetorical question expects the answer: no merely human resource can. The heart that is the seat of human identity, motivation, and aspiration is the very thing that has been most corrupted by sin — and it is not a reliable guide to its own condition. This is why biblical counseling cannot proceed simply by exploring the counselee's feelings and experiences and accepting their own account of their inner life as accurate. The Word of God — which alone penetrates to the division of soul and spirit (Hebrews 4:12) — is the only instrument capable of cutting through the layers of self-deception that sin has built.

## VII. Sin and the Fracturing of the Person

Bavinck's diagnosis of the modern condition in *Christian Worldview* — the pervasive discord between thinking and feeling, between willing and acting, between the inner life and its outward expression — is, at its root, a description of what sin has done to the integrated unity of the human person.

Before the fall, the image of God was a unity: the mind, will, and affections all worked together in harmony, each faculty properly ordered in relation to the others and all oriented together toward God. Sin shattered this unity. The conflict Paul describes in Romans 7 — "for I do not do what I want, but I do the very thing I hate" — is not the experience of an unusually disordered person; it is the universal experience of fallen humanity encountering the moral law. The person knows the good and cannot consistently perform it. The person wills what he knows will harm him. The person loves what he despises and despises what he loves. This is not a psychological puzzle; it is the spiritual consequence of sin's disruption of the integrated person.

Thomas Boston develops this in *Human Nature in Its Fourfold State* through his treatment of what he calls the *state of nature* — the condition of the unregenerate person as it actually is, prior to the work of grace. He organizes his account around four principal effects of the fall on the person:

**Spiritual death**: the unregenerate person is, in the language of Ephesians 2:1, *"dead in trespasses and sins"* — not physically dead, not incapable of biological and social functioning, but spiritually dead: without the life of God in the soul, incapable of the spiritual perception and response that genuine fellowship with God requires. As a corpse does not respond to physical stimuli, the spiritually dead person does not respond to spiritual reality — not because the reality is absent, but because the capacity for response has been extinguished by sin.

**Blindness of mind**: the understanding is so thoroughly darkened that the unregenerate person cannot see his own condition clearly, cannot perceive the beauty and authority of the gospel, and cannot evaluate spiritual reality with accuracy. This is not merely ignorance — a deficiency of information that more education might supply. It is a structural incapacity of the mind as sin has left it, which requires not merely instruction but illumination.

**Perversity of will**: the will is not merely weak but positively averse to God. Boston, following Calvin, insists that the fallen will does not stand between God and sin in a neutral position, capable of choosing either. It is inclined, by its now-corrupt nature, away from God — and this inclination is experienced not as compulsion but as genuine preference. The unregenerate person does not want God on God's terms. He may want a god who affirms him, a god who requires nothing of him, a god who can be domesticated to his own purposes — but not the living God who claims absolute sovereignty over his life and commands repentance and faith.

**Disorder of the affections**: the loves of the unregenerate person are fundamentally misdirected. The self occupies the position that God alone should occupy. Comfort, reputation, pleasure, and security are loved with the totality of devotion that belongs to the Creator. God — if acknowledged at all — is loved instrumentally, as a means to the ends the self has already chosen, rather than as the supreme good in whom all genuine good is found.

## VIII. The Deceitfulness of Sin: How Sin Hides Itself

One of the features of sin that makes it most resistant to biblical counsel is its deceiving power. John Owen, in *Of Indwelling Sin*, devotes considerable attention to what he calls sin's *deceitfulness* — its capacity to present itself under false appearances, to disguise itself as something other than what it is, and to exploit the same disordered faculties it has corrupted in order to perpetuate its hold on the person.

Owen identifies several specific mechanisms by which sin deceives. It persuades the person that the sinful object is more desirable than it actually is — inflaming the imagination and the affections before the judgment can evaluate clearly. It minimizes the seriousness of the proposed sin — suggesting that this particular transgression is small, that God will overlook it, that no real harm will follow. It presents delay as a reasonable alternative — not insisting that the sin is good but persuading the person that the moment of repentance can be deferred until a more convenient time. And it exploits the person's genuine spiritual concerns — suggesting, for instance, that acknowledgment of a particular sin would be presumptuous, or that the struggle itself is evidence that the grace of God is insufficient.

For the biblical counselor, Owen's analysis identifies one of the most persistent challenges of the counseling relationship: the person who comes for help has typically not arrived at a state of clear-eyed honesty about his own condition. He has a version of events, a set of explanations for his behavior, a way of framing his situation that protects him from the full weight of what his actions have meant and from the full demand that genuine repentance would make. The work of biblical counsel is not to strip this protective layer away violently but to bring the light of God's Word to bear with the patience, precision, and compassion that truth requires — trusting that the Spirit will do what only he can do.

## IX. The Fracture of Relationships: Sin Against God and Neighbor

Sin is not only an interior catastrophe; it is a relational one. The image of God, as Part One established, is a relational reality — the person was made for God and for others, and his dignity and vocation are only intelligible in the context of those relationships. Sin disrupts both.

The primary fracture is the fracture of the relationship with God. Romans 5:10 speaks of the unregenerate as *"enemies"* of God — not merely indifferent to him, but positively aligned against him. Colossians 1:21 describes the Gentile world as having been *"alienated and hostile in mind, doing evil deeds."* This is not merely a theological abstraction; it is the actual spiritual condition of every person before regeneration — a posture of opposition to God that may be expressed in active hostility, in apathetic indifference, or in the subtler forms of religious self-sufficiency, but that in each case represents a fundamental refusal of the relationship for which the person was made.

The secondary fracture is the fracture of the relationship with the neighbor. Jesus identifies the two great commandments as love for God and love for neighbor (Matthew 22:37–40) — and sin disrupts both. The person who does not properly love God cannot properly love his neighbor, because the neighbor is only lovable in the full sense when seen as a fellow image-bearer of the God who commands love. When the love of God is displaced by self-love, the neighbor becomes primarily a resource to be exploited, a threat to be managed, or an irrelevance to be ignored.

Genesis 3 enacts this relational fracture with tragic economy. Before the fall, Adam and Eve are together in mutual openness and trust. After the fall, they are clothed, afraid, and accusing one another before God. The first crime recorded after the fall is fratricide — Cain killing Abel. The pattern from garden to city is one of progressive relational disintegration: the fracture that sin introduced into the relationship with God reproduces itself at every level of human community.

For the biblical counselor, this means that presenting problems are rarely merely private. The person trapped in habitual sin is almost certainly also leaving a trail of relational damage — broken trust, accumulated resentments, patterns of use and manipulation that have shaped every significant relationship in his life. And the restoration that redemption offers is not merely interior — it reaches the relational life as well, restoring the capacity for the genuine love of God and neighbor that sin has disabled.

## X. What Remains: Common Grace and the Lingering Image

The doctrine of total depravity, properly understood, does not require the conclusion that nothing good remains in fallen humanity. The Reformed tradition has consistently maintained the doctrine of *common grace* — the restraining, preserving, and gift-giving work of God by which he prevents sin from running its full course in fallen humanity and preserves the capacities of the image even in its corrupted state.

Calvin's treatment of common grace in *Institutes* II.2–3 is among the most generous and careful accounts in the tradition. He insists that the remarkable intellectual, artistic, and civic achievements of fallen humanity — the philosophical wisdom of the Greeks, the political genius of the Romans, the discoveries of the sciences — are genuine goods that should be received with gratitude as gifts of the Spirit. To despise them as worthless because they come from unregenerate persons would be to insult the Spirit of God who gave them.

But Calvin is equally careful to distinguish common grace from saving grace. The gifts that common grace preserves are gifts of the image in its broader sense — the structural capacities for reason, creativity, and moral perception that persist through the fall. They do not constitute a foothold for salvation; they do not enable the person to bridge the gap between himself and God by his own effort. They are God's gift to a world that would otherwise destroy itself more rapidly than it does — a merciful restraint on the full expression of sin's destructive power.

For soul care, common grace means that the counselee is not a moral zero — not a person in whom nothing good remains and nothing can be appealed to. He has a conscience, however suppressed. He has a sense of the moral law, however distorted. He has relational instincts, however corrupted. He has, in Watson's phrase, *the ruins of a palace* — not the palace itself, but enough of the structure remaining to indicate what the building was meant to be. Biblical counseling works with what remains — not as though it were sufficient, but as the ground on which the Word of God can land and find response.

## XI. Implications for Soul Care: Why the Diagnosis Must Be Deep Enough

The doctrine of the fall and of sin's pervasive effects has a set of immediate and unavoidable implications for anyone engaged in the care of souls.

**The problem is theological before it is psychological.** Whatever presenting concern a person brings — anxiety, addiction, relational conflict, sexual sin, guilt, depression — its deepest root is in the disruption of the relationship between the person and God that sin introduced. A counseling approach that addresses the presenting concern without reaching this theological root has not addressed the real problem, however skillful its handling of the surface issues.

**Self-report is not self-knowledge.** Because the fall has corrupted the mind, disordered the affections, and enslaved the will, the counselee's own account of his condition — however sincerely offered — is not a reliable guide to what is actually happening in his soul. Biblical counsel must bring the Word of God to bear as a diagnostic instrument that reaches below the level of the counselee's self-understanding.

**Genuine change requires more than information.** The spiritually dead person cannot receive the things of God. The enslaved will cannot free itself. The darkened mind cannot illuminate itself. This means that the counselor is not the agent of genuine change — the Holy Spirit is. The counselor's work is to be a faithful instrument through whom the Spirit applies the Word. This is not a counsel of passivity; it is a counsel of precision about what human agency can and cannot accomplish in the work of soul care.

**Empathy must be joined to truth.** The Puritan tradition at its best combined a clear-eyed diagnosis of sin's depth with genuine compassion for the person caught in it. Richard Sibbes writes of Christ's tenderness toward the bruised reed — and the bruised reed is not a person who has never sinned but a person who has sinned deeply and is now broken under the weight of it. The counselor who has truly internalized the doctrine of total depravity is not a hard counselor, unmoved by the person's pain. He is a compassionate counselor who understands that the person in front of him is in the grip of something far stronger than his own will — and that the gentleness of Christ is proportional to the depth of the condition he came to heal.

**The gospel must be the destination.** Every biblical counseling session that reaches the true depth of a person's condition arrives at the same point: the need for something the person cannot supply for himself. The disruption of the image, the guilt of sin, the bondage of the will, the disorder of the affections — none of these can be addressed by human wisdom, therapeutic technique, or moral exhortation alone. They can only be addressed by the power of the gospel, applied by the Holy Spirit, through the Word of God. This is the direction toward which all faithful soul care must ultimately point.

## XII. Conclusion: The Catastrophe and the Cure

*"For all have sinned and fall short of the glory of God."* (Romans 3:23)

The magnitude of the fall is measured by the magnitude of what was lost. Part One showed us what man was — a creature made in the image of God, crowned with glory, equipped with original righteousness, oriented toward God as his supreme good, called to royal stewardship of the created order, and capable of genuine fellowship with the living God. Part Two has shown us what happened to that creature — the image defaced, the righteousness lost, the mind darkened, the will enslaved, the affections disordered, the relationship with God shattered, and the relational life with others deformed at every level.

This is not a small problem. It is not a problem that education can solve, or therapy can solve, or self-improvement can solve, or any merely human resource can solve. It is a problem that requires an intervention from outside — from beyond the fallen order, from beyond the creature's own resources, from the One who made the creature in the first place and who alone has the power and the love to restore what sin has destroyed.

That is the subject of Part Three: *Renewed After the Image — Redemption, Sanctification, and the Restored Person*. There we will see that the God who made man in his image has not abandoned the project. In Christ — the perfect image of the invisible God — he has begun a work of image-restoration that will not be completed until the final resurrection, when the children of God are fully conformed to the image of the Son.

The wound at the root is real and deep. But the Physician who comes to heal it is greater than the wound — and he leaves no case unfinished.
